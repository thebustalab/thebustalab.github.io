# AI and Science: The Full Picture

*A map of how the pieces fit together*

---

Over the past year I have written a handful of posts about artificial intelligence in science, each one prompted by a question I kept hearing from students, colleagues, and audiences after talks. When should I reach for these tools? If they already know my field, why do I need to? What about the environmental cost? The pieces look like separate subjects, but a single argument runs beneath every one of them. This post is the map — short by design, with a paragraph on each piece and a pointer to the full discussion.

The premise behind all of it: AI is already part of how science gets done, so the useful question is no longer whether to use it but how to use it well. Answering that well means holding several things in mind at once — and the posts below take them one at a time.

## When should a scientist use AI?

The first piece sketches a decision framework, not a rulebook. Most people fall into one of two camps: those who have folded AI into nearly every task without asking whether each use is appropriate, and those who refuse it outright and forgo real gains on low-risk work. The middle ground rests on a few ideas — you own whatever carries your name, a human in the loop only counts when that person can actually catch what the model gets wrong, and deep training in your field is the real amplifier. The move that ties them together is decomposition: most tasks are really several tasks wearing one label, and each part deserves its own judgement about whether you can check the result. Read the full piece: [When Should a Scientist Use AI?](../ai_decision_flow/)

## Why does expertise still matter?

The framework leans on something it cannot supply: the expertise that lets you validate what AI produces. The second piece makes the case that deep training matters *more* in an AI-assisted world, not less. Expertise is not a store of facts — it is judgement under uncertainty, the ability to see what is important rather than merely what is typical, which is precisely where a statistical model is weakest. There is a trap here, though: the tools can wear away the very skill they depend on, because skill is built through the practice we are tempted to hand off. At the scale of a whole field, that erosion shows up as narrowing and as distorted signals about who is good at what. The way out is deliberate training — careful sequencing, assessment you can actually observe, and protection for the work that stays human for now. Read the full piece: [Disciplinary Expertise in an AI-Assisted World](../ai_and_domain_expertise/)

## What about the environmental cost?

The third piece separates two questions that usually get tangled. One query costs about as much energy as running a microwave for two seconds, so the footprint of an individual researcher's use is, by any honest accounting, negligible. The aggregate demand from data centres is a different story — real, growing, and worth taking seriously — but it is a structural matter of grids, chips, and policy rather than a problem any one person solves by typing less. The guilt framing most people arrive with points them at the wrong lever. The better response is intentional use paired with action at the level where it counts: how the electricity is generated and how the build-out is governed. Read the full piece: [The Environmental Cost of AI](../ai_environmental_impact/)

## So what do we do in the classroom?

The next piece, still to come, turns the expertise argument into concrete advice for teaching chemistry: which tasks students should work through by hand and why, how to reform assessment so it measures understanding rather than output, and how to sequence a course so the foundations are built before the shortcuts are allowed. *(Forthcoming.)*

## And what does the university look like in ten years?

The last piece, also forthcoming, takes the long view. Scientific work has layers — execution, analysis, interpretation, design, and direction — and automation is climbing them from the bottom. The catch is that the upper layers depend on having worked through the lower ones; you cannot interpret a result well without having generated many yourself. That tension defines the durable role of a university: teaching people to think about the work, not only to carry it out. I weigh a few models for doing that — the medical-residency approach, a design-and-interpret approach, and hybrids between them. *(Forthcoming.)*

## The through-line

Each post answers a different question, but the same claim sits underneath all of them: **the more AI can do, the more it matters that the people using it have genuine expertise — and the harder that expertise becomes to build if we are not deliberate about how we train for it.** The decision framework needs expertise to work. Expertise is what erodes if we delegate carelessly. The classroom and the university are where that expertise is either protected or lost. Even the environmental question resolves into a matter of judgement about where individual effort actually matters.

These posts do not replace one another, and this one does not replace them. It is the map that shows how they connect.

A closing note: this is a moving target. The tools change month to month, the numbers get revised, and my own thinking will keep shifting as the evidence does. Treat what is here as a snapshot of an argument still being worked out, not a final word — I expect to redraw parts of this map before long.
