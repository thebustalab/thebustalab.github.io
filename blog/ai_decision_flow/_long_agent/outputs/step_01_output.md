# When Should a Scientist Use AI?

*A framework for thinking about it — not a set of rules*

---

There is a widening gap in how scientists relate to AI. On one side are researchers who have woven it into nearly every task without pausing to ask whether each use is appropriate. On the other side are colleagues who refuse to engage at all. Both positions carry costs. Uncritical adoption risks producing work that looks right but hasn't been properly examined; blanket rejection means forgoing genuine productivity gains on tasks where AI performs well and the risks are low.

There is also a practical reason to care about getting this right. Scientists are busy — genuinely, structurally busy. If AI can reliably handle some of the mechanical work — the reformatting, the boilerplate, the first-pass organisation — then the time reclaimed goes back to irreducibly human activities: reading deeply, thinking carefully, mentoring, collaborating. But that bargain only works if the AI use is thoughtful. Careless adoption erodes the very expertise that makes the tools worthwhile.

This post is my attempt to find a principled middle ground — a framework for deciding when AI is and isn't a good fit for tasks in the natural sciences, grounded in actual research workflows. It grew out of conversations with students and colleagues, and out of watching (and making) both good and bad decisions about when to reach for these tools.

A note on scope: this post focuses on *when* and *how* to use AI responsibly. It does not address the environmental costs of AI — energy consumption, water use, and carbon footprint. Those are real and worth taking seriously, and I plan to address them in a [separate post]().
