### The two sides together

This is the core tension at the individual level: AI both requires and erodes expertise. It amplifies what you know, but it can prevent you from developing that knowledge in the first place — or degrade knowledge you already had. The implication is that AI use cannot be all-or-nothing. It has to be deliberate: some tasks delegated, others done by hand specifically because the doing is what builds the capacity to judge.

A concept from labour economics clarifies where the vulnerability lies. A job is not a single task — it is a *bundle* of tasks. Some bundles are weak: the components can be cheaply separated, and the separable parts are the ones AI takes over. Other bundles are strong: the tasks are so interdependent that pulling one out degrades the rest. An experienced scientist's work is a strong bundle — experimental design, data interpretation, mentoring, and judgement about what to pursue next all feed into each other. But a graduate student's work, early in training, is a weaker bundle. Much of it — literature summaries, routine data processing, first-pass code — *can* be separated and handed to AI. The uncomfortable implication is that the career stage most vulnerable to AI disruption is also the one where you are building the interdependencies that make you indispensable later. If AI hollows out the junior bundle too aggressively, the pipeline that produces people with strong bundles breaks.

Krishnan (2026) argues that PhD training should establish foundational mastery benchmarks *before* AI tools are introduced for core tasks. The apprenticeship has to come before the delegation.

A 2026 study reinforces this logic: *fluent* AI users — those with deep expertise and comfort with the tools — actually experience more failures than novices, but their failures are visible, recoverable, and occur alongside greater success on complex tasks. Expertise lets you know when AI has failed, which may be the single most important meta-competency in AI-assisted work. Novices do not fail less; they fail without noticing.
