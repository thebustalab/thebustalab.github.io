## Part two: what happens across a field

The individual-level picture is important, but it is not the whole story. When many researchers adopt AI simultaneously, emergent effects appear at the population level that no single individual intended or controls.

### The steering problem

A large-scale *Nature* study (2025), analysing 41.3 million papers, found that AI-using scientists published 67% more papers and received roughly three times more citations than non-users. But across 70% of sub-fields studied, AI collectively *contracted* the range of topics investigated. Papers clustered around computationally tractable, data-rich problems; follow-on engagement between papers dropped by 24%. The researchers describe a "star-like structure" — many papers orbiting the same hot topics, high output but low engagement between distinct lines of work.

This is what happens when productivity is decoupled from the judgement that decides what is worth being productive about. AI shifts the path of least resistance — not just for individuals, but for entire fields. Without enough scientists who can recognise what *matters* as distinct from what is statistically tractable, research drifts toward local maxima: problems that are easy to work on rather than problems that are important to solve.

The steering problem is not a deskilling problem — it can happen even among experts. But it is amplified when erosion reduces the number of researchers with the deep judgement needed to resist the pull of tractable problems. The two levels interact: individual erosion feeds the collective narrowing of science.

### Population-level effects

Two further emergent patterns compound the steering problem. The first is what Lin and Sohail call the "complacency cascade": users initially scrutinise AI outputs carefully, then gradually accept them with less critical evaluation, particularly for technical content where detecting subtle errors requires concentrated attention. At the individual level, complacency is a personal risk. At the population level, it becomes systemic — collective quality degrades not through any single dramatic failure, but through a thousand small acceptances of output that was almost right.

The second is distorted evaluation. If career metrics reward publication output, and AI inflates that output without corresponding growth in expertise, then hiring and promotion panels are evaluating a systematically distorted signal. Cotton and Scholle-Cotton warn this could create leadership gaps — researchers who rose quickly on AI-assisted productivity but lack the deep understanding to guide a research programme or recognise when a promising line of work is headed for a dead end. Even a scientist who personally manages the balance between AI use and skill development operates in a system where the metrics that once served as rough proxies for expertise — publications, citations, grant productivity — have been decoupled from understanding.
