### The unwritten dimension

The erosion problem runs deeper than textbook skills. A large fraction of what makes a scientist expert is *unwritten knowledge* — how to read a chromatography peak, when to suspect contamination, how to recognise that a reaction flask looks wrong before you can say exactly why. Sometimes called *tacit knowledge*, it is learned through practice, observation, and years of experience, not instruction.

This knowledge is transmitted through mentoring — years of working alongside someone more experienced, absorbing their standards for what counts as "good enough" evidence. AI cannot currently access it because it was never captured in any training corpus. A 2025 preprint on AI laboratory agents acknowledged this directly: "Current AI tools have proven effective in literature analysis and code generation, but do not address the critical gap between documented knowledge and implicit lab practice."

Even if unwritten knowledge is eventually digitised, the erosion concern remains. What matters is not the information but the reflexes built through practice. The chemist who notices a colour change while her conscious attention is elsewhere is operating on thousands of hours of wired-in pattern recognition — and those reflexes are not confined to the bench. The PI who designs a strong experiment draws on years of running experiments, including failed ones, that have given her an instinct for what will work and what controls are needed. The reviewer who spots a flaw in a methods section draws on the same embodied sense. Unwritten knowledge is not just useful for laboratory work — it is the substrate on which higher-order scientific judgement is built.

This makes it a particularly vulnerable casualty of the erosion paradox. If trainees delegate bench work, first-pass analyses, and hands-on troubleshooting, they may never develop the embodied fluency that feeds into every other part of their scientific thinking. Explicit skills can be re-learned from a textbook. The unwritten dimension cannot.
