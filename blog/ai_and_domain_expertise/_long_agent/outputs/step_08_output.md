## What this means for how we train scientists

If AI amplifies expertise but can erode it through the same mechanism, produces population-level effects no single researcher controls, and the unwritten dimension remains the hardest to build and easiest to lose — then how we develop expertise deserves serious attention. A few implications follow.

**The case for more rigorous disciplinary training, not less.** Routine tasks were often the vehicle through which deeper understanding was built. Not every task needs to be done the hard way — but some do, and knowing which ones requires the expertise of the mentor.

**Deliberate sequencing matters.** AI tools should enter the curriculum only after students have demonstrated they can do the core work independently — otherwise there is nothing to validate against.

**Assessment needs to shift toward observable reasoning.** If AI can produce polished outputs indistinguishable from expert work, a polished output is no longer strong evidence of understanding. Assessment should move toward formats where reasoning is visible: oral defences, live problem-solving, and structured decision reviews.

**Protect time for what is — for now — irreducibly human.** Time reclaimed by AI should go toward mentoring conversations, collaborative thinking, sitting with puzzling results, and reading deeply rather than broadly. In an AI-assisted world, these *are* the real work — and building the human capacity to do them well remains the foundation for using AI responsibly.

**Resist the narrowing.** Funding bodies can specifically support data-sparse, anomaly-rich, and foundationally oriented research that AI tends to deprioritise. PIs can protect space for questions chosen because they are important, not because they are easy to work on with current tools.
