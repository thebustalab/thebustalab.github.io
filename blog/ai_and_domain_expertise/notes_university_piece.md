# University Future Directions — Working Notes

*Separate piece from the disciplinary expertise post. Different audience: colleagues, administrators, institutional decision-makers.*

---

## Core question

How should universities respond to AI's effects on scientific training and research — in the near term, in 10 years, and beyond?

## Key ideas from the discussion

### Near term (next few years)
- Most universities haven't adjusted curricula at all
- The instinct will be to reduce disciplinary content ("students can just use AI for that") — this is the wrong move
- The right move is restructuring: keep the rigour, shift emphasis from rote execution toward judgement and experimental design
- **Assessment reform is the most urgent piece** — if evaluation doesn't change, AI-assisted polished output masks whether anyone is learning
- Deliberate sequencing: foundational mastery benchmarks before AI tools are introduced

### Medium term (~10 years)
- Fundamental question: training people to *do* scientific work vs. to *direct and evaluate* it?
- Those require overlapping but different skill sets
- The directing/evaluating role puts a premium on judgement, question selection, ethical reasoning
- But you probably can't build the judgement without doing the execution first (surgical training analogy)
- So even if the end-state role is directorial, the training path likely still goes through extensive hands-on work

### Institutional dynamics
- If AI narrows tractable research topics (Hao et al. Nature 2025), data-sparse / fieldwork-heavy / bench-intuition fields may struggle for students and funding
- Those may be exactly the fields where important unsolved questions live
- Universities need to actively protect those areas rather than letting allocation follow path of least resistance
- Career metrics (publications, citations) being distorted by AI-inflated output — hiring/promotion signals becoming unreliable (Cotton & Scholle-Cotton 2026)
