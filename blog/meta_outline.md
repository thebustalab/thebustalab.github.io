# Meta Post — AI and Science: The Full Picture

*Skeleton outline — weaves the individual posts into a single narrative arc*

---

## The arc

- AI is here in science. The question isn't whether to use it — it's how to use it well. That requires thinking clearly about several things at once.

## The story in sequence

- **When should a scientist use AI?** (Post 1)
  - Not everything should be delegated. A decision framework: confidentiality, mode, decomposition, validateability, responsibility.
  - The key insight: most tasks are not one task. Break them apart, evaluate each piece.

- **But the framework depends on something it can't provide: expertise.** (Post 2)
  - AI amplifies what you know — the centaur model, the force multiplier.
  - But it can erode the very expertise it needs. The erosion paradox. Unwritten knowledge built through practice. Strong vs weak bundles.
  - At the population level: science narrows, complacency cascades, evaluation signals distort.
  - Training implications: deliberate sequencing, observable assessment, protecting what's (for now) irreducibly human.

- **And what about the environmental cost of all this?** (Post 3)
  - Individual use: negligible, sometimes lower-emission than the alternative.
  - Aggregate: real and growing, but structural — not a behaviour problem.
  - The usual guilt framing misleads. Intentional use, not abstinence. Career-level action on grid and policy.

- **So what do we actually do in the classroom?** (Future post: chemistry courses)
  - Concrete recommendations for chemistry instruction.
  - Which tasks students do by hand and why. Assessment reform. Sequencing.

- **And what does the university look like in ten years?** (Future post: university training)
  - The layers of scientific work: execution → analysis → interpretation → design → direction.
  - Lower layers automating (Medra, AI scientists). Upper layers depend on having worked through lower ones.
  - The durable role of universities: training people to think about the work, not just do it.
  - Medical residency model, hybrid model, design-and-interpret model.

## The through-line

- Each post answers a different question, but the same argument runs underneath all of them: **the more AI can do, the more it matters that the humans using it have genuine expertise — and the harder it becomes to build that expertise if we're not deliberate about it.**
- The meta post doesn't replace the individual pieces — it's the map that shows how they connect.

## Possible structure for the meta post itself

- The through-line stated explicitly
- A closing that looks forward: this is a moving target, and the thinking will evolve
